7–26. WITNESS OPINION ON CREDIBILITY OR GUILT

NOTE 1: Lay or expert witness testimony that may be confused with an opinion on credibility, guilt or innocence. When a lay or expert witness has expressed an opinion that might be construed as an opinion concerning the credibility of the alleged victim or other witness or that an offense was or was not committed, the following instruction may be given immediately after that witness testifies, and then repeated in the closing instructions:

Only you determine the credibility of the witnesses and what the facts of this case are. No (expert) witness can testify that (the alleged victim’s) (a witness’s) account of what occurred is true or credible, that the (expert) witness believes (the alleged victim) (another witness), or that (a crime) (a sexual offense) (__________) occurred. To the extent that you believe that (state the name of witness) testified or implied that (he) (she) believes (the alleged victim) (a witness), that a crime occurred, or that (the alleged victim) (a witness) is credible, you may not consider this as evidence that a crime occurred or that (the alleged victim) (witness) is credible.

NOTE 2: Where expert's belief in victim's allegations offered for a proper, limited purpose. In limited cases, an expert may testify that he/she believed a victim to explain why the expert treated a victim or acted in a certain way. In such cases, the following may be appropriate and given immediately after the instruction following NOTE 1 above:

You may, however, consider it only for the limited purpose of explaining why (state the name of expert) acted as (he) (she) did (in providing care or treatment to (state the name of alleged victim)) (__________).

REFERENCES: Lay testimony as a human lie detector impermissible: US v. Knapp, 73 MJ 33 (CAAF 2014); US v. Jackson, 74 MJ 710 (ACCA 2015).

Expert testimony as a human lie detector impermissible: US v. Armstrong, 53 MJ 76 (CAAF 2000); US v. Birdsall, 47 MJ 404 (CAAF 1998); US v. Harrison, 31 MJ 330 (CMA 1990).

Expert testimony comparing victim's behavior / symptoms with known characteristics of victims permissible: US v. Birdsall, 47 MJ 404 (CAAF 1998); US v. Suarez, 35 MJ 374 (CMA 1992); US v. Harrison, 31 MJ 330 (CMA 1990).
