Elements

Please take a look at look at the specification __ of Charge __, which represents a violation of Article 128 of the Uniform Code of Military Justice. The elements of that offense, which is called aggravated assault – intentionally inflicting grievous bodily harm, are:

That (stated the time and place alleged), you inflicted grievous bodily harm, that is: (state the injuries inflicted), upon (victim);

That you did so by (state the manner alleged);

That the grievous bodily harm was done with unlawful force or violence; and

That you, at the time, had the specific intent to inflict grievous bodily harm; and

GIVE THE FOLLOWING ELEMENT IF A LOADED FIREARM IS ALLEGED:

That the injury was inflicted with a loaded firearm.

IF THE ALLEGED VICTIM WAS A CHILD, GIVE THE FOLLOWING ELEMENT:

That at the time of the assault, (victim) was a child under the age of 16 years.

Definitions

In considering those elements, you should use and understand the following definitions:

An “assault” is an attempt or offer with unlawful force or violence to do bodily harm to another. An assault in which bodily harm is inflicted is called a battery.

A “battery” is an unlawful and intentional application of force or violence to another. The act must be done without legal justification or excuse and without the lawful consent of the victim. “Bodily harm” means any physical injury to or offensive touching of another person, however slight.

Grievous bodily harm means fractured or dislocated bones, deep cuts, torn members of the body, serious damage to internal organs, or other serious bodily injuries.

This offense requires the actual infliction of grievous bodily harm.

Additionally, the grievous bodily harm must have been intentionally caused by you, that is, you must have had, at the time of the assault described in the specification, a specific intent to cause serious bodily injury.

Light pain, minor wounds, and temporary impairment of some organ of the body do not ordinarily, either individually or collectively, establish grievous bodily harm. These results are common to most ordinary assault and battery cases.

GIVE THE FOLLOWING DEFINITION IF A LOADED FIREARM IS ALLEGED

“Firearm” means any weapon which is designed to or may be readily converted to expel any projectile by the action of an explosive. A handgun rifle shotgun, when used as a firearm and not as a club, may not be considered a dangerous weapon or means likely to produce death or grievous bodily harm unless it is loaded. A fully functional revolver with an automatic rotating cylinder is a loaded weapon if there is a round of live ammunition in any chamber. A functional clip or magazine fed weapon is a loaded weapon if there has been inserted into it a clip or magazine containing a round of live ammunition, regardless of whether there is a round in the chamber.

POTENTIAL DEFENSES

SELF DEFENSE

NOTE: An accused cannot use force which is likely to produce death or grievous bodily harm to defend against an assault that will inflict only bodily harm. Self-defense is a defense though if the accused believed he/she was subject to death or grievous bodily harm by the “victim.” See benchbook instructions 5-2-1 and 5-2-2.

Selfdefense would be a complete defense to this offense.

For selfdefense to exist you must have had a reasonable apprehension that death or grievous bodily harm was about to be inflicted on you and you must have actually believed that the force you used was necessary to prevent death or grievous bodily harm.

In other words, the defense of selfdefense has two parts. First, you must have had a reasonable belief that death or grievous bodily harm was about to be inflicted on you. The test here is whether, under the same facts and circumstances present in this case, an ordinary, prudent adult person faced with the same situation would have believed that there were grounds to fear immediate death or serious bodily harm. Because this test is objective, such matters as your intoxication or any emotional instability on your part are not relevant.

Secondly, you must have actually believed that the amount of force you used was required to protect against death or serious bodily harm. As long as you actually believed that the amount of force you used was necessary to protect against death or grievous bodily harm, the fact that you may have used excessive force (or a different type of force than that used by the attacker) does not matter.

DEFENSE OF ANOTHER

A person may use force in defense of another only if that other person could have lawfully used such force in defense of (himself/herself) under the same circumstances. (Therefore, if (state name of person defended) (was also an aggressor) (intentionally provoked an attack) (was a mutual combatant) then you could not lawfully use force in (his/her) behalf (regardless of your understanding of the situation).)

For defense of another to exist, you must have had a reasonable belief that death or grievous bodily harm was about to be inflicted on the person defended, and, you must have actually believed that the force you used was necessary to protect that person.

In other words, defense of another has two parts. First, you must have had a reasonable belief that death or grievous bodily harm was about to be inflicted on (state name of person defended). The test here is whether, under the same facts and circumstances, a reasonably prudent person, faced with the same situation, would have believed that death or grievous bodily harm was about to be inflicted.

Second, you must have actually believed that the amount of force you used was necessary to protect against death or grievous bodily harm.

As long as you actually believed that the amount of force you used was necessary to protect against death or grievous bodily harm, the fact that you may have used such force (or a different type of force than that used by the attacker) does not matter.
