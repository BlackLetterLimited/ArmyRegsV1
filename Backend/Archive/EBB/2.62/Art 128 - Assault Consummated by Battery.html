Elements

Please take a look at look at the specification of Charge I, which represents a violation of Article 128 of the Uniform Code of Military Justice. The elements of that offense, which is called assault consummated by a battery, are:

(1) That (stated the time and place alleged), you did bodily harm to (victim);

(2) That you did so by (state the manner alleged); and

(3) That the bodily harm was done with unlawful force or violence.

Definitions

In considering those elements, you should use and understand the following definitions:

An “assault” is an attempt or offer with unlawful force or violence to do bodily harm to another. An assault in which bodily harm is inflicted is called a battery. A “battery” is an unlawful and intentional (or) (culpably negligent) application of force or violence to another. The act must be done without legal justification or excuse and without the lawful consent of the victim. “Bodily harm” means any physical injury to or offensive touching of another person, however slight.

“Culpable negligence” is a degree of carelessness greater than simple negligence. “Simple negligence” is the absence of due care. The law requires everyone at all times to demonstrate the care for the safety of others that a reasonably careful person would demonstrate under the same or similar circumstances; that is what “due care” means. “Culpable negligence,” on the other hand, is a negligent (act) (or) (failure to act) accompanied by a gross, reckless, wanton, or deliberate disregard for the foreseeable results to others.

POTENTIAL DEFENSES

PARENTAL DISCIPLINE

NOTE 2: Parental discipline can constitute an affirmative defense. However the right of a parent to discipline a child by use of force is not without limits. When the defense of parental discipline is raised, the military judge should instruct as follows:

A parent does not ordinarily commit a criminal offense by inflicting corporal punishment upon a child subject to (his/her) parental authority because such parental authority includes the right to discipline a child. The corporal punishment must be for the purpose of safeguarding or promoting the welfare of the child, including the prevention or punishment of the child’s misconduct, and the force used may not be unreasonable or excessive.

“Unreasonable or excessive force” is that designed to cause or known to create a substantial risk of causing death, serious bodily injury, disfigurement, extreme pain, extreme mental distress, or gross degradation.

If your act(s) in (striking) (___________) your child (was) (were) for the purpose of disciplining the child, and the force used was not unreasonable or excessive as I have defined those terms, you are considered to have had legal justification for your acts and are not guilty. However, if at the time of your act(s), you were motivated by other than a parental desire to safeguard or promote the welfare of your child, including the prevention or punishment of misconduct, or, that the force you used was unreasonable or excessive, then the act(s) are not excusable as permissible, parental disciplinary measures.

NOTE 3: Who may claim the defense. This defense may also be employed by a guardian or other person similarly responsible for the child’s general care and supervision or a person acting at the request of a parent, guardian, or other responsible person. When the evidence raises the issue of whether the accused may avail himself or herself of this defense, the military judge must present this issue to the members. The following may be helpful:

You are authorized to discipline a child if you are the child’s parent, guardian, one similarly responsible for the general care and supervision of the child, or acting at the request of a parent, guardian, or other responsible person.

Was your purpose in (striking) (___________) your child for the purpose of disciplining the child?

Were you (victim)’s parent, guardian, one similarly responsible for the general care and supervision of the child?

(If not) Were you acting at the request of a parent, guardian, or other responsible person?

Was the force you used unreasonable or excessive, as I’ve defined those terms?

Tell me why you believe the force you used was unreasonable or excessive.

SELF DEFENSE

NOTE 4: If the accused answers that he / she was acting in self defense, use this instruction. (This instruction is distinguished from deadly force situations. When ordinary assault or battery is charged and deadly force is not employed, the standard of selfdefense is different from a situation in which deadly force is employed. The accused must only apprehend some bodily harm, not death or grievous bodily harm. However, when the accused only apprehends some bodily harm, the accused is then limited in the force which the accused can legitimately use to defend himself or herself, i.e., the accused may not use such force as would likely cause death or grievous bodily harm.)

Selfdefense would be a complete defense to this offense.

For selfdefense to exist you must have had a reasonable belief that bodily harm was about to be inflicted on you and you must have actually believed that the force you used was necessary to prevent bodily harm.

In other words, the defense of selfdefense has two parts. First, you must have had a reasonable belief that physical harm was about to be inflicted on you. The test here is whether, under the same facts and circumstances in this case, any reasonably prudent person faced with the same situation would have believed that (he/she) would immediately be physically harmed. Because this test is objective, such matters as your intoxication or any emotional instability on your part are not relevant.

Secondly, you must have actually believed that the amount of force you used was required to protect yourself. In protecting yourself, you are not required to use the same amount or kind of force as the attacker. However, you cannot use force which is likely to produce death or grievous bodily harm.

“Grievous bodily harm” means serious bodily injury. It does not mean minor injuries such as a black eye or a bloody nose, but does mean fractured or dislocated bones, deep cuts, torn members of the body, serious damage to internal organs, or other serious bodily injuries.

At the time you did bodily harm to (victim), did you believe he / she was about to assault you?

Did you believe that physical harm was about to be inflicted on you?

(If yes) Do you believe that a reasonably prudent person would have believed the same?

Did you believe that the amount of force you used was required to protect yourself?

(If yes) Did you use force which was likely to produce death or grievous bodily harm?

NOTE 5: Reasonableness of apprehension of harm. The ordinary objective standard used to determine whether apprehension of serious bodily harm or death was reasonable must be qualified if there is evidence of a special factor affecting the reasonableness of the apprehension (e.g., sex of the accused, age of the accused, or if the accused is a person who lacks sufficient intelligence to act as a normal prudent adult person). The requirement of reasonableness should be determined in light of these special factors.

NOTE 6: Selfdefense—opportunity to withdraw—presence of others. The accused is not required to retreat when at a place the accused has a right to be. The presence or absence of an opportunity to withdraw may be a factor in deciding whether the accused acted in selfdefense. The following instruction should be given when opportunity to withdraw or the presence of others is raised by the evidence.

A person may stand (his/her) ground when (he/she) is at a place at which (he/she) has a right to be. Under the pressure of a fast moving situation or immediate attack, you are not required to pause at your peril to evaluate the degree of danger or the amount of force necessary to protect yourself. The violence and rapidity, if any, involved in the incident should be considered.

Did you have a right to be at the place of the incident?

Were you standing your ground?

MUTUAL COMBAT

NOTE 7: Provocateur—mutual combatant. One who intentionally provokes an assault, or voluntarily engages in mutual combat is not entitled to claim selfdefense, although the right to selfdefense may be regained by good faith withdrawal. The following instructions may be used, as appropriate, in conjunction with earlier instructions, when such issues are raised by the evidence. If any of the following instructions are given, the instruction following NOTE 9 is ordinarily required.

A person who (intentionally provoked an attack upon (himself/herself)) (voluntarily engaged in mutual fighting) is not entitled to selfdefense (unless (he/she) previously withdrew in good faith) (unless it was physically impossible for (him) (her) to withdraw in good faith) (unless the adversary escalated the level of conflict).

A person has provoked an attack and, therefore, given up the right to selfdefense if (he/she) willingly and knowingly does some act toward the other person reasonably calculated and intended to lead to a fight (or a deadly conflict). Unless such act is clearly calculated and intended by you to lead to a fight (or a deadly conflict), the right to selfdefense is not lost.

(A person may seek an interview with another in a nonviolent way for the purpose of (demanding an explanation of offensive words or conduct) (demanding redress of a grievance or settlement of a claim) without giving up the right to selfdefense. One need not seek the interview in a friendly mood. (The right to selfdefense is not lost merely because the person arms (himself/herself) before seeking the interview.))

Had you intentionally provoked an attack upon yourself?

Did you voluntarily engage in mutual fighting?

(If yes to either) Did you withdraw from that encounter in good faith at some point?

(If no) Was it physically impossible for you to withdraw for some reason?

(If no) Did your adversarly escalate the level of the conflict?

(If applicable) How did the other person respond to your withdrawal from the fight? (Did they continue the fight?)

NOTE 8: Withdrawal as reviving right to selfdefense. The following instruction covers the burden of proof when there is an issue of withdrawal.

Even if you (intentionally provoked an attack upon yourself) (voluntarily engaged in mutual fighting), if you later withdrew in good faith and indicated to your adversary a desire for peace, by words or actions or both, and if (victim) (followed you and) revived the (conflict) (fight), then you were no longer (voluntarily engaged in mutual fighting) (provoking an attack) and were entitled to act in selfdefense.

How did you indicate your withdrawal?

DEFENSE OF ANOTHER

Did you believe that (victim) was about to assault someone else?

Whom do you think was about to be assaulted?

A person may use force in defense of another only if that other person could have lawfully used such force in defense of (himself/herself) under the same circumstances. (Therefore, if (state name of person defended) (was also an aggressor) (intentionally provoked an attack) (was a mutual combatant) then you could not lawfully use force in (his/her) behalf (regardless of your understanding of the situation).)

For defense of another to exist, you must have had a reasonable belief that bodily harm was about to be inflicted on the person defended, and, you must have actually believed that the force you used was necessary to protect that person, and the force used by you must have been less than force likely to result in death or grievous bodily harm.

In other words, defense of another has two parts. First, you must have had a reasonable belief that bodily harm was about to be inflicted on (state name of person defended). The test here is whether, under the same facts and circumstances, a reasonably prudent person, faced with the same situation, would have believed that bodily harm was about to be inflicted.

Second, you must have actually believed that the amount of force you used was necessary to protect against bodily harm, and the force used by you was not likely to cause death or grievous bodily harm.

In defending another person you are not required to use the same type or amount of force used by the attacker, but you cannot use force which is likely to produce death or grievous bodily harm.

Was (state name of person defended) also (an aggressor) (a mutual combatant) (or did (person defended) intentionally provoke an attack upon themselves)?

(If no) Do you believe that a reasonably prudent person would have believed that bodily harm was about to be inflicted on (state name of person defended)?

Did you believe that the amount of force you used was required to protect (state name of person defended)?

(If yes) Did you use force which was likely to produce death or grievous bodily harm?
