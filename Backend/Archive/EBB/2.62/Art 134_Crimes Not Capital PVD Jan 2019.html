Article 134 – CRIMES NOT CAPITAL

Elements:

NOTE 2: Pursuant to FY 2017 NDAA, Article 134 is amended and specifically provides that under clause 3, extraterritorial jurisdiction exists over non-capital federal crimes committed outside the United States which include as an element that the crime occur “within the special maritime or territorial jurisdiction of the United States.”.

NOTE 3: Identifying elements and applicable definitions. The military judge should ordinarily seek the position of counsel as to the elements and applicable definitions and hold an Article 39(a) session early in the trial to clarify generally what instructions may be given. Federal pattern instructions are available online from the United States Court of Appeals Library.

NOTE 4: Terminal Element. The specification must expressly allege, and the military judge must instruct as a separate element, that the charged Federal offense is not a capital offense.

(1) That (state the time and place alleged), you did or failed to do certain acts, namely ____________________;

(2) That, by doing or failing to do said acts, the you satisfied every element of a federal offense, specifically (state U.S. Code provision alleged);

(3) The elements of the federal crime alleged are: (list elements);

(4) That (U.S. Code provision alleged) was in effect on DATE; and

(5) That the offense alleged is a crime not capital.

Definitions:

An act is “wrongful” if done without legal justification or excuse.

NOTE 5: Other definitions must be provided as required based on the offense alleged.

Inquiry:

0a: Are you currently on active duty in the U.S. Navy/U.S. Marine Corps?

0b: On [DATE OF EARLIEST OFFENSE] were you a member of the U.S. Navy/U.S. Marine Corps on active duty?

0c: Have you been discharged or released from active duty since that date?

0d: In the specification is your name, rank, rate, unit, and branch of service correctly stated and spelled?

0e: Do you understand the elements and definitions as I have read them to you?

0f: Do you have any questions about any of them?

0g: Do you understand that your plea of guilty admits that these elements accurately describe what you did?

0h: Do you believe and admit that the elements and definitions taken together correctly describe what you did?

0i: Tell me in your own words why you believe you are guilty of this offense.

1a: On DATE at PLACE did you do or fail to do certain acts, namely ACTS OR FAILURES ALLEGED?

1b: Explain the circumstances surrounding the acts you did or failed to do.

2a: By doing or failing to do said acts, do you believe and admit that your conduct violated (U.S. Code provision alleged)?

2b: How did you violate (U.S. Code provision alleged)?

3a: Do you believe and admit that you actions or failures to act satisfied every element of (U.S. Code provision alleged)? HERE THE JUDGE MUST GO OVER EVERY ELEMENT AND THE MANNER IN WHICH THE CONDUCT OF THE ACCUSED SATISFIED IT.

4a: Do you believe and admit that (U.S. Code provision alleged) was in effect on DATE?

4b: Have you discussed this with your counsel?

5a: Was (U.S. Code provision alleged) a crime not capital.

5b: Did anyone force you to act or fail to act in this manner?

5c: Do you have any legal justification or excuse for your action or failure to act?

5d: Could you have avoided acting or failing to act in this manner if you had wanted to?

5e: Do you believe and admit that your conduct was wrongful, that is, without any legal justification or excuse?
