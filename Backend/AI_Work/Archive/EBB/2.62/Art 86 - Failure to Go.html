POTENTIAL ISSUES raised?

Control by civilian authorities ordinarily is not a defense, but see MCM Part V ¶ 10c(5).

Deliberate Avoidance Raised? If so, instruct as follows:

You must have known that you were required to be present at the appointed time and place of duty.

You may not, however, willfully and intentionally remain ignorant of a fact important and material to your conduct in order to escape the consequences of criminal law. Therefore, if you were aware that there was a high probability that you were required to be present at an appointed time and place of duty; and you deliberately and consciously tried to avoid learning that you were required to be present at an appointed time and place of duty, then this raises the issue that you deliberately avoided positive knowledge. Such deliberate avoidance of positive knowledge is the equivalent of actual knowledge.

In other words, if you had your suspicions aroused that you were required to be present at a certain place of duty at a time prescribed, but then deliberately omitted making further inquiries because you wished to remain in ignorance, that meets the knowledge requirement.

I emphasize, however, that knowledge cannot be established by mere negligence, foolishness, or even stupidity on your part. Rather, you may not affirmatively choose to remain ignorant when you knew the information was available and you could ascertain the necessary knowledge. Consequently, if you had actual knowledge that you were required to be present at the appointed time and place of duty, or you deliberately avoided that knowledge, as I have defined that term, then you may plead guilty.
