Article 128B – DOMESTIC VIOLENCE – VIOLATION OF A PROTECTIVE ORDER WITH INTENT TO COMMIT A VIOLENT OFFENSE

Elements:

ARTICLE 128b – DOMESTIC VIOLENCE – VIOLATION OF A PROTECTIVE ORDER WITH INTENT TO COMMIT A VIOLENT OFFENSE

NOTE: The President did not define the term “violent offense” until 26 January 2022, upon signing Executive Order 12473. Accordingly, for Article 128b (Domestic Violence—Violent Offense) offenses that occurred on or after 1 January 2019, but before 26 January 2022, military judges should proceed with caution and should consider appropriately tailoring the instructions or the plea colloquy. See United States v. Gloverstukes, 2023 CCA Lexis 59.

Elements:

(1) That (state the time alleged) a lawful protection order was in place;

(2) That (state the time and place alleged) you committed an act in violation of that lawful protection order by (state the manner in which the order was allegedly violated); and

(3) That you committed the act with the intent to commit a violent offense against your (spouse) (intimate partner) (immediate family member).

Definitions:

The term “protection order” means (a) a military protective order enforceable under 10 U.S.C. Section 892 (Article 92, UCMJ); or (b) a protection order, as defined in 18 U.S.C. Section 2266 and, if issued by a State, tribal, or territorial court, is in accordance with the standards specified in 18 U.S.C. Section 2265.

(An order enforceable under 10 U.S.C. Section 892 means:

(1) That (state the name and rank or grade of the person issuing the order or regulation), a member of the armed forces, issued a lawful order, to wit: (state the date and specific source of the alleged order and quote the order or the specific portion thereof);

(2) That the accused had knowledge of the order; and

(3) That the accused had a duty to obey the order.

An order, to be lawful, must relate to specific military duty and be one that is authorized under the circumstances. An order is lawful if it is reasonably necessary to safeguard and protect the morale, discipline, and usefulness of the members of a command and is directly connected with the maintenance of good order in the services. It is illegal if it is unrelated to military duty; its sole purpose is to accomplish some private end; it is arbitrary and unreasonable; or it is given for the sole purpose of increasing the penalty for an offense which it is expected the accused may commit.)

(A protection order, as defined in 18 U.S.C. Section 2266, means:

(A) any injunction, restraining order, or any other order issued by a civil or criminal court for the purpose of preventing violent or threatening acts or harassment against, sexual violence, or contact or communication with or physical proximity to, another person, including any temporary or final order issued by a civil or criminal court whether obtained by filing an independent action or as a pendente lite order in another proceeding so long as any civil or criminal order was issued in response to a complaint, petition, or motion filed by or on behalf of a person seeking protection; and

(B) any support, child custody or visitation provisions, orders, remedies or relief issued as part of a protection order, restraining order, or injunction pursuant to State, tribal, territorial, or local law authorizing the issuance of protection orders, restraining orders, or injunctions for the protection of victims of domestic violence, sexual assault, dating violence, or stalking.)

(Under 18 U.S.C. Section 2265, a protection order issued by a State, tribal, or territorial court meets the standards specified if:

(1) such court has jurisdiction over the parties and matter under the law of such State, Indian tribe, or territory; and

(2) reasonable notice and opportunity to be heard is given to the person against whom the order is sought sufficient to protect that person’s right to due process. In the case of ex parte orders, notice and opportunity to be heard must be provided within the time required by State, tribal, or territorial law, and in any event within a reasonable time after the order is issued, sufficient to protect the respondent’s due process rights.)

The term “violent offense” includes a violation of (state the appropriate alleged violent offense from the list below):

(a) Article 118, UCMJ,

(b) Article 119(a), UCMJ,

(c) Article 119a, UCMJ,

(d) Article 120, UCMJ,

(e) Article 120b, UCMJ,

(f) Article 122, UCMJ,

(g) Article 125, UCMJ,

(h) Article 126, UCMJ,

(i) Article 128, UCMJ,

(j) Article 128a, UCMJ,

(k) Article 130, UCMJ, or

(l) Any other offense that has an element that includes the use, attempted use, or threatened use of physical force against the person or property of another.)

(The term “spouse” means one’s husband or wife by lawful marriage.)

(The term “intimate partner” means either (a) one’s former spouse, a person with whom one shares a child in common, or a person with whom one cohabits or with whom one has cohabited as a spouse; or (b) a person with whom one has been in a social relationship of a romantic or intimate nature, as determined by the length of the relationship, the type of relationship, and the frequency of interaction between the persons involved in the relationship.)

(The term “immediate family” means either (a) one’s spouse, parent, brother or sister, child, or other person to whom he or she stands in loco parentis; or (b) any other person living in one’s household to whom he or she is related by blood or marriage. “In loco parentis,” meaning “in place of a parent,” is a legal doctrine describing a relationship similar to that of a parent to a child; it refers to an individual who assumes parental status and responsibilities for another individual, usually a young person, without formally adopting that person.)

Inquiry:

0a: Are you currently on active duty in the U.S. Navy/U.S. Marine Corps?

0b: On [DATE OF EARLIEST OFFENSE] were you a member of the U.S. Navy/U.S. Marine Corps on active duty?

0c: Have you been discharged or released from active duty since that date?

0d: In the specification is your name, rank, rate, unit, and branch of service correctly stated and spelled?

0e: Do you understand the elements and definitions as I have read them to you?

0f: Do you have any questions about any of them?

0g: Do you understand that your plea of guilty admits that these elements accurately describe what you did?

0h: Do you believe and admit that the elements and definitions taken together correctly describe what you did?

0i: Tell me in your own words why you believe you are guilty of this offense.

1a: On DATE was there a lawful protection order in place?

1b: Was the order a military protective order or a protection order issued by a court?

[If MPO]

- Who issued the order?

- What were the terms of the order?

- Were you aware of the order?

- How did you become aware?

- Approximately when did you become aware of it?

- Did the order relate to a specific military duty? What duty?

- Was it reasonably necessary to safeguard and protect the morale, discipline, and usefulness of the members of a command? How so?

- Do you agree that the order was directly connected with the maintenance of good order in the military?

- Do you agree that it was authorized under the circumstances?

- Do you admit that the order was lawful?

- Did you have a duty to obey the order?

[If protection order]

- Has your defense counsel gone over with you the definition of a protection order contained in 18 USC Section 2266?

- Based on that conversation, are you satisfied that the protection order you received meets the definition contained in 18 USC Section 2266?

- How so?

- What was the purpose of the order?

- What were the terms of the order?

- Was the order issued by a State, tribal, or territorial court?

- Did that court have jurisdiction over you at the time of the order?

- Were you given notice of the order?

- How did you learn of the order?

- Were you given an opportunity to respond to the order?

- Did you respond to the order?

- Do you admit that the order was lawful?

- Did you have a duty to obey the order?

2a: On or about DATE at PLACE did you fail to obey the order?

2b: Tell me about how you violated the order?

2c: Were there any exceptions to the order? (If so) Did your situation fit any of them?

2d: Did anyone authorize you to violate the written order?

2e: Did anyone or anything force you to violate it?

2f: Do you believe you had any legal justification or excuse for what you did?

2g: Could you have complied with the written order if you had wanted to?

3a: Why did you violate the order by committing ACT?

3b: Did you have the intent to commit a violent offense? Describe the violent offense.

3c: At the time of the offense, was the person you intended to commit a violent offense against (your spouse) (your intimate partner) (your immediate family member)?

3d: How do you know this?
